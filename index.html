Getting it Through Your Head: How Biomedical Engineers are Using Nanoparticles and Focused Ultrasound to Treat Brain Tumors

Introduction

You probably have not heard of biomedical engineers, but you use their inventions every single day. From the tomato in your sandwich to your leggings or the annual flu shot, biomedical engineering is everywhere! The role of a biomedical engineer is to apply scientific discoveries and technology towards solving problems in health.

A major issue in health is cancer. Living creatures are made up of small subunits, called cells. Cancer is a disease where a person’s cells mutate and start dividing and growing uncontrollably. This can result in large growths of mutated cells, called a tumor. It is very likely you know of someone who has had cancer, it is estimated that around 2 in 5 Canadians will be diagnosed with cancer during their lifetime (). Cancer is also the leading cause of death in the world ().

A particularly difficult type of cancer to treat is tumors in the brain. Brain tumors are difficult to treat because… Biomedical engineers have been focusing on solving this problem, researching and developing ways to specifically target cancer cells without harming the brain. A new approach that might solve this problem is a combination of two technologies: nanoparticles and focused ultrasound.

On the other hand, focused ultrasoundFocused ultrasound is a noninvasive therapeutic technology with the potential to transform the treatment of many medical disorders by using ultrasonic energy to target tissue deep in the body without incisions or radiation. (commonly abbreviated as FUS) is a newer technology which can vibrate, heat up, and even destroy tissue in a targeted area. Have you ever held a magnifying glass outside on a sunny day? If you have, you’d notice the sun converges to one single point in the middle - it’s focused. The sun by itself may feel warm on your skin, but that focused point can actually set things on fire! This is roughly analogous to how focused ultrasound works, but this time, we are using very high frequency sound instead of light. These focused sound waves can pass through skin without harm, but come to a focused point inside the body. One common use case is to use focused ultrasound to damage a tumor in a controlled manner - this is typically referred to as ablationThe removal or destruction of a body part or tissue or its function.
