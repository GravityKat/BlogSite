Getting it Through Your Head: How Biomedical Engineers are Using Nanoparticles and Focused Ultrasound to Treat Brain Tumors

Introduction

You probably have not heard of biomedical engineers, but you use their inventions every single day. From the tomato in your sandwich to your leggings or the annual flu shot, biomedical engineering is everywhere! The role of a biomedical engineerAn engineering professional who combines their knowledge of biology and technology to design sustainable solutions to issues related to healthcare is to apply scientific discoveries and technology towards solving problems in health.

A major issue in health is cancerA disease that happens when cells begin dividing and growing uncontrollably. Living creatures are made up of small subunits, called cellsThe microscopic subunits which make up all life. Cancer is a disease where a person’s cells mutate and start dividing and growing uncontrollably. This can result in large growths of mutated cells, called a tumorA mass of cancer cells. It is very likely you know of someone who has had cancer – it is estimated that around 2 in 5 Canadians will be diagnosed with cancer during their lifetime [1]. Cancer is also the leading cause of death in the world [2].

A particularly difficult type of cancer to treat is tumors in the brain. Brain tumors are difficult to treat because: 1) it is difficult to detect brain tumors early on, 2) it is difficult to remove the tumors through surgery without damaging the brain, and 3) current cancer drugs cannot access the brain tumors due to the blood-brain barrierSpecial cells which form a tight barrier to filter blood going into the brain. The blood-brain barrier is a layer of very tightly spaced cells, which blocks the transport of certain molecules between the blood and the brain [3].

Biomedical engineers have been focusing on solving these two problems, researching and developing ways to 1) specifically target cancer cells without harming the brain and 2) allow cancer drugs to access tumors by bypassing the blood-brain barrier. A new approach that might solve these problems is a combination of two technologies: focused ultrasound and nanoparticlesUltrasound but stronger and concentrated, which can vibrate, heat up, and even destroy tissue.

Background

Focused What Now?

When you hear the word “ultrasound”, you might think of one of those cone-shaped black and white grainy pictures of you or a loved one as a baby, still growing inside the womb. This is an example of ultrasound imagingOne way of looking inside the body using sound.

Source: Ultrasound Of Baby In Pregnant Woman

On the other hand, focused ultrasoundUltrasound but stronger and concentrated, which can vibrate, heat up, and even destroy tissue (commonly abbreviated as FUS) is a newer technology that can vibrate, heat up, and even destroy tissue in a targeted area. Have you ever held a magnifying glass outside on a sunny day? If you have, you’d notice the sun converges to one single point in the middle - it’s focused. The sun by itself may feel warm on your skin, but that focused point can actually set things on fire! This is roughly analogous to how focused ultrasound works, but this time, we are using very high frequency sound instead of light. These focused sound waves can pass through skin without harm, but come to a focused point inside the body. One common application is to use focused ultrasoundUltrasound but stronger and concentrated, which can vibrate, heat up, and even destroy tissue to damage a tumor in a controlled manner - this is typically referred to as ablationControlled destruction of a tumor in the body [4].

Source: Eye safety when starting a fire with a magnifying glass

Source: An Introduction to High Intensity Focused Ultrasound: Systematic Review on Principles, Devices, and Clinical Applications

FUS combines the metaphorical “sun” and “magnifying glass” into a single piece of technology designed by biomedical engineers, medical researchers, biologists, and physicists. This piece of technology is known as a FUS transducerA device that produces focused ultrasound (shown above). In particular, this type of FUS transducer is known as a concave focusing transducerA FUS transducer which relies on geometry to focus the ultrasound, which is called “concave” by the shape of the face of the device. There is ongoing research for lenses that focus sound (acoustic lenses) to make the device cheaper, which would make the device work more like how the magnifying glass focuses the sun [5]. However, this isn’t the typical setup in FUS.

However, there is one downside to the standard concave focusing transducer. It is fixed-focusReferring to a transducer which will always focus ultrasound in the same place relative to the device, which means the focused part of the ultrasound is always at the same place relative to the device, and the size of the focused part is always the same. While typically more expensive, the phased array transducerA transducer which uses clever timing to adjust the focus and position of the delivered focused ultrasound design allows the focus to be changed. This allows for better precision during treatment, as we don’t want to damage healthy cells! The focus can also be moved without moving the actual device, in contrast with the concave focusing transducer which needs to be physically moved to ablate a different part of the tumor. The phased array transducer incorporates clever timing with many ultrasound elements (even thousands at a time!) to create constructive interferenceWhen two waves overlap they can form a stronger wave which is the addition of the two of ultrasound at the specified focal point. One way to think of it is like this: The ultrasound wave has a certain speed, and a single element has a certain position. If we want the waves of two emitters to arrive at the same time, then since they are not at the same position, one must fire before the other, and the time can be calculated based on distance.

Source: High intensity ultrasound phased array for surgical applications | Semantic Scholar

An example of a phased array transducer in development comes out of Sunnybrook Research Institute in Toronto, led by Dr. Kullervo Hynynen [6]. This device is essentially a helmet that can deliver FUS to any part of the brain, which will be super useful for new brain disease treatments!

Surprisingly, FUS can do much more than ablate tissue when used at different power levels. At lower power, it can non-destructively vibrate cells in a way. That’s why it is also being investigated for drug delivery. Our brain is an amazing organ, and it has a defense mechanism to filter out harmful substances in the blood that go to our brain. This defense mechanism is called the blood brain barrierSpecial cells which form a tight barrier to filter blood going into the brain (BBB). However, if someone has a brain disease like a tumor, this can prevent a potentially life saving drug from getting to the tumor. Where does FUS come in? Dr. Hynynen’s group discovered that with FUS, in combination with microbubblesVery small bubbles which can vibrate and oscillate when exposed to focused ultrasound (very small bubbles invisible to the naked eye) injected into the bloodstream, the BBB can be temporarily [6]. The microbubbles vibrate rapidly, opening up gaps in between the cells that make up the BBB. This literally opens doors for drug delivery to the brain! But which drugs would we use, and why?

Small But Mighty

Typically, when someone has cancer, they will undergo chemotherapy. However, many chemotherapy drugs are not specific - they end up hurting the good cells too, which is why you see many lose hair during treatment. This lack of specificityThe ability of a drug to target the desired area and not damage unwanted areas can have substantial negative effects on the health of unaffected tissue and cells in the brain’s vasculature and microenvironment components [7].

Have you heard of nanoparticlesVery small carriers of drugs which can be engineered to have a specific target or survive in certain conditions? They are essentially extremely small carriers of drugs. Nanoparticles can be one solution to specificity. They can be used as controlled drug delivery systemsA method of getting a drug to go exactly where we want it to go, typically at a specified concentration and time [8]. Through careful design of the nanoparticles, they can target specific cells, like tumors.

Let’s walk through an analogy to make this a bit more digestible. Our non-specific chemotherapy drug is like blanket mailing - those random flyers you get in your mailbox. Some of the time, they may be useful to someone, but most of the time, they’re delivered to people who don’t even need them, just clogging up the mailbox! On the other hand, nanoparticles are like a very efficient delivery system - for example Amazon. You order something through an app because you need it, and it arrives right to you the very next day. Nanoparticles can be designed such that they can navigate the body and only target the tumor [10].

A Happy Marriage

Remember our friend, the great protector of the brain, the blood-brain barrier? The Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) is designed to protect the brain from harmful substances such as toxins, pathogensSmall organisms which cause diseases, like bacteria or viruses among other things from entering brain tissue with ease. However, when a brain tumor develops, this vasculatureThe system of blood vessels which transport oxygen and nutrients to a specific area of the body. can adopt new characteristics that are referred to as the Brain Tumor BarrierWhen a brain tumor develops, it causes changes to characteristics of the Blood-Brain Barrier. This is called the Brain Tumor Barrier. (BTB). Often BTB’s can be “leakier” than the unaffected BBB. In other cases of brain cancer, the BBB remains intact and limits the transport of chemotherapy drugs from accessing the tumor sites [9].

The blood-brain barrier will generally block nanoparticles from reaching the brain. Biomedical engineers and researchers have thought of a great idea, and this is where FUS comes in, which can selectively open the blood-brain barrier and allow our nanoparticles to traverse their way into the depths of the brain and target the tumor. A phased array device like K. Hynynen’s helmet could facilitate accurate targeting of FUS. This can improve the efficacy of chemotherapy treatment of brain tumors as well as minimize the negative effects of using these harsh agents for healthy tissue. This overall approach is much less invasive and safer than an open brain surgery, which could contribute to better outcomes in brain cancer patients.

Purpose

Why is This Important?

Now that we have learned a bit about FUS and nanoparticles, we hope you understand how exciting this technology is for treating brain tumors! Brain tumors are the most common solid tumor in children, and the most deadly type of cancer [3]. As well, brain tumor patients may experience loss of physical, social, and mental capabilities, which makes it difficult for patients to work and live independently [11]. This can put a lot of stress on the patient and their families. Using the combination of FUS and nanoparticles to treat brain tumors will improve the treatment efficiency and the quality of life for patients. It is likely that this will result in higher survival rates from all stages of cancer, as the drugs will not be affecting healthy tissues including those involved in immune response.



When this technology becomes more common in cancer treatment, this may also lower healthcare costs since a large portion of cancer treatment costs come from the need for more frequent and prolonged treatment [12]. In the long run for each patient, costs will be much lower as this technology will result in more rapid treatment and prognosis programs. Stories of successes and recoveries may spread and increase awareness of such treatment options and technologies to further standardized and engrain FUS and nanoparticles into cancer treatment, which may encourage innovation in broadening the application of such technology and improve upon it.

It is also important to note that treating cancer can consume a lot of resources and be harmful to the environment. It is estimated that the healthcare industry represents 10% of greenhouse gas emissions in the United States [13]. Creating more effective treatments like FUS and nanoparticles will help decrease the length of treatment and as such reduce harmful emissions and waste associated with cancer treatment.

Role of Biomedical Engineers

Biomedical engineers are responsible for not only designing the technical solutions for complex healthcare issues, but also doing their due diligence to investigate the impacts of their solution on social, economic, and environmental scales. Rigorously testing their designs against the requirements of stakeholdersPeople who affect and are affected by a problem which requires an engineering solution. The needs and wants of stakeholders influence the solution the engineers design. and evaluating the solution’s compliance with legal guidelines and regulations, biomedical engineers have a responsibility to ensure the safety and practicality of their solutions for the good of the public.



For this particular technology, global organizations like Health Canada and the Food and Drug AdministrationNational regulatory bodies that decide the guidelines for medical technologies and evaluate whether new technologies can be used on the public. (FDA) outline guidelines for therapeutic nanoparticles [14], [15]. These regulations are to ensure that the nanoparticles are safe and effective medicines. This is done by testing the drug activity of the nanoparticles in the lab and also in clinical trials. These regulations also tell manufacturers how to handle nanoparticles to prevent potential risks of unintended exposure to people and the environment. International regulations for nanoparticles are still being constantly debated and updated [16]. As such, only a small number of nanoparticles have been approved by regulatory agencies so far. This is because there are many ways to create different nanoparticles which all need to be properly investigated, and regulatory agencies are still working on understanding what tests are most useful for judging the safety and effectiveness of nanoparticles [17]. Biomedical engineers developing nanoparticles need to consider these regulations and can help provide thorough data to regulatory agencies to help guide the development of these regulations.



As for focused ultrasound, the FDA also has guidelines and regulations to evaluate the specific applications of FUS. For example, the FDA has approved the use of focused ultrasound to treat neurological disorders like tremors. However, focused ultrasound to treat brain tumors is still in the clinical trial phase [18], [19]. Designing with these guidelines in mind is a major consideration for biomedical engineers to ensure their solutions are safe for the public.

Sustainability

So far, we went into great detail explaining how combining FUS and lipid nanoparticles to treat brain tumors works and how it affects our society. But there’s an important question we have to ask before actually implementing this technology into the real world: “Can this technology be applied sustainably?” Before we answer this question, let’s define what sustainability means first. The United Nations defines sustainability as “meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” [20]. The term ‘needs’ here can refer to all sorts of needs, like physical needs to stay healthy, financial needs to buy things, and ecological needs to preserve the environment. In other words, we need to make sure that by treating brain tumors with FUS and lipid nanoparticles, we aren’t negatively affecting future generations in a societal, economic, and ecological way.

Source: 3 Pillars of Sustainability

Societal Sustainability

Let’s first revisit the societal impact of brain tumors - having a tumor grow inside someone’s brain means their everyday lives will be made much more difficult because they might forget things and their personality could change [21]. This will not only impact the patients but also their family and friends in their social circle; imagine your loved ones forgetting who you are and how devastating that could be. By implementing this new technology, we can expect the quality of life for both patients and their close connections to improve [21][22]. But does that mean this technology is available for everyone in society? Most likely not. Here’s why that is and what we can do to fix it.

Source: Patient and Caregivers’ Quality of Life

Nanoparticles play such an important role in this treatment, but let’s remember that they may not work for everyone. The sad news is that your loved ones and other patients may be screened out by doctors for health reasons. Remember that nanoparticles do not naturally form in a person’s body, so their immune systemthe natural defense system of the body that protects against harmful germs and any other agents that can cause sickness might think it's harmful like an outside germ and decide to fight back. As a result, doctors and officials may decide that only certain people can be considered for this treatment while the people who do not meet the requirements are left out. For example, COVID-19 vaccines use lipid nanoparticles(a specific type of nanoparticle) to make sure the medicine spreads throughout your body but some people cannot safely get it because of their age, medical history, and even allergies [23]. Similar to how different types of COVID-19 vaccines were recommended for different age groups, this new treatment needs to explore different ways to make different types of lipid nanoparticles [24]. Nanoparticles can be made in a lab with a protocol, much like making a favorite dish with a recipe. Therefore, it is important to continuously experiment with the recipe to make nanoparticles, even as this treatment is being used, in order to make sure that different types of nanoparticles are discovered for people that may not be able to safely get this treatment right now. Doing this will make sure that this type of brain surgery is sustainable in the long run because more people will be able to benefit from it!

Economic Sustainability

People often think more effective disease treatment can only benefit the patients – they’re the ones getting healthier, right? But introducing FUS and nanoparticles to treat brain tumors can help the economy grow too! Here’s why.

Every time a patient gets checked by their doctor, it costs money and someone has to pay for it – either the government with taxpayer money or the patients themselves [25]. But more effective treatments let patients stay healthier for longer and need fewer follow-up visits to the doctor’s office (cite). That means by decreasing the total number of check-ups needed, FUS and nanoparticles can decrease the total cost to treat brain tumors [25]. Imagine this lower cost offered to every brain tumor patient – its economic impact will grow even greater!

But wait, what happens when not everyone gets equal access to this technology? After all, we come from different economic backgrounds and live in a world full of price tags. Studies show that wealthier people are far more likely to receive better healthcare treatments than poor people [26]. On top of that, brain surgeries are already very expensive as is (without FUS and nanoparticles involved), with patients paying approximately 75,000 CAD in Canada [26]. That’s almost as much as the median income for Canadian households [27]! If we were to implement this new technology without any financial support and ask the patients to pay for everything out of their pocket, most might not even be able to afford it. To prevent this technology from being an exclusive treatment option for the wealthy, it should be implemented in a way where the healthcare system actively looks for and reaches out to patients who need the technology [28]. This way, the healthcare system can act as a ‘safety net’ to protect brain tumor patients who need this treatment to survive but are unable to afford it on their own [29].

Source: Financial Inequality in Healthcare

Ecological Sustainability

You might be wondering why a technology to treat brain tumors can have anything to do with the environment. How could brain tumor surgeries happening inside hospital doors affect trees growing in a provincial park? Here’s how.

Hospitals order lots of medical supplies – like surgical tools, equipment, and drugs – to provide patients with the treatments they need [30]. Since most of these supplies are produced from all around the world, they often need to be transported by airplanes [31]. And as we all know, transportation is one of the industries that produce the most carbon dioxide (or CO2), contributing to climate change [32]. On top of that, nanoparticles needed for this technology usually need to be kept at very low temperatures (around -80 °C) to work best, during both shipping and storage [33][34]. Let’s assume a company from the USA just made a fresh batch of nanoparticles, packed them in dry ice for cold storage, and shipped them off to a hospital in Europe via an airplane. How much CO2 do you think will be released to the environment?

More than 87,000 kg of CO2 - that’s a lot [34][35]!

Source: Financial Inequality in Healthcare

Moving nanoparticles that need to be frozen at around -80 °C is very bad for the environment right now. However, it is also a perfect example of a problem that needs to be fixed for the future. Fortunately, smart scientists are already working on different ways to store nanoparticles or extend their shelf life which will be great for our planet! Having nanoparticles with longer shelf lives means that more of them can be shipped in a single delivery and healthcare professionals won’t need to restock as often. Less deliveries means less planes and other CO2 emitting vehicles are being used! Already there is some research that is saying some labs are able to make nanoparticles that can last 18 weeks in 25 °C in phosphate buffered salinea liquid used in labs, particularly useful in biological experiments (PBS) without cryoprotectantsa helpful chemical used to protect organics from the harmful effects of freezing [36]. While the scientists behind this research are unsure why there can be such a large difference in shelf lives, they are saying it is important to look further into cryoprotectantsa helpful chemical used to protect organics from the harmful effects of freezing, buffersa chemical that protects against extreme changes in acidity or basicity, and storage temperatures to determine the exact reason [36]. For the sake of having a greener planet this type of research is absolutely necessary to continue and consider when moving forward with this type of treatment.
