Getting it Through Your Head: How Biomedical Engineers are Using Nanoparticles and Focused Ultrasound to Treat Brain Tumors

Introduction

wordThis is the definition of the word.

You probably have not heard of biomedical engineerdefinitions, but you use their inventions every single day. From the tomato in your sandwich to your leggings or the annual flu shot, biomedical engineering is everywhere! The role of a biomedical engineer is to apply scientific discoveries and technology towards solving problems in health. A major issue in health is cancerdefinition. Living creatures are made up of small subunits, called cellsdefinition. Cancer is a disease where a person’s cells mutate and start dividing and growing uncontrollably. This can result in large growths of mutated cells, called a tumordefinition. It is very likely you know of someone who has had cancer – it is estimated that around 2 in 5 Canadians will be diagnosed with cancer during their lifetime. Cancer is also the leading cause of death in the world. A particularly difficult type of cancer to treat is tumors in the brain. Brain tumors are difficult to treat because: 1) it is difficult to detect brain tumors early on, 2) it is difficult to remove the tumors through surgery without damaging the brain, and 3) current cancer drugs cannot access the brain tumors due to the blood-brain barrierdefinition. The blood-brain barrier is a layer of very tightly spaced cells, which blocks the transport of certain molecules between the blood and the brain. Biomedical engineerdefinitions have been focusing on solving these two problems, researching and developing ways to 1) specifically target cancer cells without harming the brain and 2) allow cancer drugs to access tumors by bypassing the blood-brain barrier. A new approach that might solve these problems is a combination of two technologies: focused ultrasound and nanoparticlesdefinition.
